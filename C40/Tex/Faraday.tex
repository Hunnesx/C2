\section{Measuring the Faraday effect inside a glass body}

In the Faraday effect, the plane of polarisation of linearly polarised light is rotated while propagating through a 
material placed inside a magnetic field. For an approximately constant magnetic field, the following relation holds:

\[
\Theta = V \cdot B_{\mathrm{eff}} \cdot l
\]

where $\Theta$ is the measured rotation angle, $V$ the Verdet constant, $B_{\mathrm{eff}}$ the effective magnetic 
flux density, and $l$ the length of the glass sample.

In this experiment, a glass body with a length of $l = (30.0 \pm 0.5)\,\mathrm{mm}$ was investigated.

\subsection{Determination of the Effective Magnetic Field}

First, the magnetic field along the optical axis was measured using a Hall probe. Since the magnetic field inside 
the solenoid is not perfectly homogeneous, an effective magnetic field was determined from the measured field profile.

The effective magnetic field is defined as:

\[
B_{\mathrm{eff}} = \frac{1}{l}\int_0^l B(x)\,dx
\]

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth]{../Images/Figure_1_1.png}
    \caption{Magnetic flux density $B(x)$ as a function of the position $x$ along the optical axis.}
    \label{fig:magnetic_field_profile}
\end{figure}

From this, the effective magnetic field is determined to be:
\[
B_{\mathrm{eff}} =(69,7 \pm 1,1)\,\mathrm{mT}
\]

based on the maximum magnetic field ,$B_{\mathrm{max}} = (96,1 \pm 0.05)\,\mathrm{mT}$, the following value is obtained 
for the parameter $\alpha$:

\[
\alpha = \frac{B_{\mathrm{eff}}}{B_{\mathrm{max}}} = 0,725 \pm 0,012
\]

This factor is later used to determine the effective magnetic field for other coil currents from the measured maximum 
magnetic field. Therefore, we assume that $\alpha$ remains constant for different currents, as the magnetic field geometry 
should not change with the glass sample in place.

\subsection{Magnetic flux density per current}

Afterwards, the effective magnetic flux density was determined for different currents. Plotting $B_{\mathrm{eff}}$
as a function of the current $I$ reveals an approximately linear dependence.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth]{../Images/Figure_1_2.png}
    \caption{Maximal flux densities for different currents. The proportionality constant between the strongest magnetic 
    flux density and the solenoid current is determined by conducting a linear regression.}
    \label{fig:magentic_flux_density_per_current}
\end{figure}

As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:magentic_flux_density_per_current}, $B_{\mathrm{max}}$ exhibits an approximately linear 
dependence on $I$. Therefore, a linear function was fitted, yielding the slope to be $C_B = \frac{dB_{\mathrm{max}}}{dI}= (46,245 \pm 0.013)\,\mathrm{mT/A}$.


\subsection{Faraday Rotation per current}

For different wavelength ranges, the rotation angle $\Theta$ of the plane of polarisation was measured as a function 
of the effective magnetic field $B_{\mathrm{eff}}$.

The analyser was rotated until the signal of the photodiode reached a minimum. The corresponding angle was interpreted 
as the rotation angle caused by the Faraday effect.

For each wavelength range, a linear function fit was performed.

\subsection{Calculation of the Verdet Constant}

Using the relation

\[
\Theta = V B_{\mathrm{eff}} l
\]

the Verdet constant can be calculated from the slope of the linear fits:

\[
V
=
\frac{\Theta}{B_{\mathrm{eff}}l}
\]

Since the slope $m$ is obtained in units of $\frac{^\circ}{\mathrm{mT}}$, a conversion into SI units is necessary:

\[
V
=
m
\cdot
\frac{\pi}{180}
\cdot
1000
\cdot
\frac{1}{l}
\]

with

\[
l = 0.030\,\mathrm{m}
\]

The calculated Verdet constants are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:verdet}.

\subsection{Dispersion of the Verdet Constant}

Finally, the Verdet constant was plotted as a function of the mean wavelength of the corresponding interference filter.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth]{../Images/Figure_1_4.png}
    \caption{Dispersion of the Verdet constant as a function of the wavelength.}
    \label{fig:verdet_dispersion}
\end{figure}



The measurements show that the Verdet constant decreases with increasing wavelength. This behaviour is expected for 
dispersive optical media such as N-SF6 glass. Shorter wavelengths experience a stronger rotation of the plane of polarisation 
than longer wavelengths.

The data point corresponding to the wavelength range 360--460 nm should be interpreted carefully, since several measured values 
deviate more strongly from the linear behaviour. Possible reasons include reduced light intensity in this wavelength range, 
alignment uncertainties, or thermal effects caused by heating of the coils.


The Faraday effect was successfully observed for all investigated wavelength ranges. In each case, an approximately linear 
dependence between the rotation angle and the effective magnetic field was found.

The calculated Verdet constants satisfy the qualitative relation

\[
V_{360-460}
>
V_{400-560}
>
V_{560-630}
\]

which means that the Verdet constant decreases with increasing wavelength, as expected from theory.
