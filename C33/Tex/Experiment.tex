\section{Experiment}

    \subsection{Measurements on Leslie's cube} \label{sec:SBLeslie}
    

    For the following measurements the setup shown in figure \ref{fig:setup} was used.
    This consists of a Leslie's cube on a turntable which is heated by a thermostat. The cube has four different surfaces: a white, a black, a shiny nickel-plated and a matte nickel-plated. A thermopile is used to measure the thermal radiation emitted by the different surfaces of the cube. The thermopile is connected to a voltmeter which displays the voltage generated by the thermopile.
    The filtermount can be used to place different filters in front of the thermopile, which will be done in section \ref{sec:Ultrarotdurchlässigkeit}.
    The errors of the temperature and the voltage are calculated according to the specifications of the thermostat Lauda Kältethermostate RE 104 and the voltmeter Agilent 34405A (\cite{thermostat}, \cite{Voltmeter}).
    \begin{figure}[H]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{../Images/setup.png}
        \caption{Experimental setup for measurements on Leslie's cube.}
        \label{fig:setup}
    \end{figure}

        \subsubsection{Stefan-Boltzmann-law}

        Figure \ref{fig:linLeslie} shows the voltage generated by the thermopile as a function of the temperature of the cube for the different surfaces. The fit used is of the form $U = A (T^b - T_0^b)$, where $T_0 = 293.15 K$ is the reference temperature measured via the zero point of the voltmeter. The fit parameters are shown in table \ref{tab:SBBlackbody}.

        \begin{figure}
            \centering
            \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{../Images/individual_fits_U(T).png}
            \caption{Voltage generated by the thermopile as a function of the temperature of the cube for the different surfaces. The fit used is of the form $U = A (T^b - T_0^b)$. With $T_0 = 293.15 K$ as the reference temperature.}
            \label{fig:linLeslie}
        \end{figure}
        \noindent
        As one can see in figure \ref{fig:linLeslie} the black side emits the most thermal radiation and the polished nickel-plated side emits the least, which is expected since the black surface has the highest emissivity and the polished nickel-plated surface has the lowest emissivity. 
        The data of the polished nickel-plated surface is very noisy. This matches with it's fit exponent $b$ which is far off aswell, this will be discussed further below in more detail.

        \begin{table} [H]
            \centering
            \begin{tabular}{c|c}
                Surface & fit exponent $b$ \\
                \hline
                White & $3.770 \pm 0.059$ \\
                Black & $4.043 \pm 0.081$ \\
                Polished nickel-plated & $2.8 \pm 2.3$ \\
                Matte nickel-plated & $3.94 \pm 0.21$ \\
            \end{tabular}
            \caption{The exponents determined by the $U = A (T^b - T_0^b)$ fit to the data obtained by measurements on Leslie's cube.}
            \label{tab:SBLeslie}
        \end{table}
        \noindent
        The expected values of the exponents is $b=4$ according to the Stefan-Boltzmann law. The values obtained for the black and matte nickel-plated surfaces match 
        that within their error, while the value obtained for the white and polished nickel-plated surface are a bit off.
        The value obtained for the white surface is close to the expected value, but the error is small enough to exclude $b=4$. The value obtained for the polished 
        nickel-plated does match $b=4$ within the error, but the error is very large which is a good representation of the poor quality of the measurement. 
        This poor quality is due to the fact that the polished side reflects a lot of thermal radiation from the environment, which is also measured by the thermopile 
        and can not be easily seperated from the radiation emitted by the surface itself. A solution to this would be to place the setup in a more contained environment 
        and shield it further from external radiation. \\

        The voltage is also plotted as a function of the forth power of the temperature as you can see in figure \ref{fig:SBLeslie2}. 
        \begin{figure}
            \centering
            \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{../Images/individual_fits_U(T4).png}
            \caption{Voltage generated by the thermopile as a function of the forth power of the temperature of the cube for the different surfaces. The fit used is of the form $U = A (T^4 - T_0^4)$, where $T_0$ is the room temperature.}
            \label{fig:SBLeslie2}
        \end{figure}
        \noindent
        Here one can see that all sides follow a linear trend, which is expected. The slope of the nickel-plated surfaces is much smaller than the slope of the white and black surfaces, which is also expected since they have a much lower emissivity.
        

        \subsubsection{Infrared permeability for several materials} \label{sec:Ultrarotdurchlässigkeit}

        In this part of the experiment the setup in figure \ref{fig:setup} was reused, but only the black side of the cube was used, now with 
        different optical filters placed in front of the thermopile. The used filters were: window glass, silicon and sodium chloride. \\
        The temperature was set to $T = 77^\circ$C with an environmental temperature of $T_{0} = 20^\circ$C. The thermal radiation emitted by the black surface
        was measured first, after that the filters were placed in front of the thermopile and the measurements were repeated. 
        The results are given in percentages of the voltage measured without a filter and shown in table \ref{tab:filters}.
        
        \begin{table}
            \centering
            \begin{tabular}{c|c}
                Filter & infrared permeability (\%)\\
                \hline
                Window glass & $2.27 \pm 0.48$ \\
                Silicon & $46.82 \pm 0.48$ \\
                Sodium chloride & $73.32 \pm 0.48$ \\
            \end{tabular}
            \caption{Transmittance of infrared radiation through different filters at $T = 77^\circ$C.}
            \label{tab:filters}
        \end{table}
        \noindent
        It shows that thus although the window glass transmits visible light very well, the infrared transmittance is the lowest of the three. 
        Since glass is an amorphous material, it has a lot of vibrational modes which can be excited by the infrared radiation, which leads 
        to a high absorption and thus low transmittance. Silicone however lets almost half of the infrared radiation through, 
        which is due to the fact that it is a crystalline material with covalent bonds and to be more precise, it is a indirect band gap semiconductor.
        This means that it has a band gap which can be excited by the infrared radiation, but for cristal-momentum conservation, a phonon also needs 
        to be involved, which is less likely to happen than just exciting the band gap, which leads to a higher transmittance than glass but not as high as 
        sodium chloride. Sodium chloride has the highest transmittance of the three, which can be referred to the fact that it is an ionic crystal with a 
        large band gap, which can not be excited by the infrared radiation, leading to a very low absorption and thus high transmittance. \\
        \noindent
        With Wien's displacement law one can also calculate the wavelength of the maximum spectral intensity of the thermal radiation:
        \begin{equation}
            \lambda_{max} = \frac{b}{T}
        \end{equation}
        where $b = 2897.771955 \mu m * K$ is Wien's displacement constant and $T$ is the temperature of the black surface in Kelvin. 
        For the measured room temperature of $T_0 = 20^\circ$C and the temperature of the black surface of $T = 77^\circ$C, 
        one obtains a wavelength of $\lambda_{max,0} = (9.885 \pm 0.033 \mu m)$ and $\lambda_{max,leslie} = (8.276 \pm 0.020 \mu m)$ respectively.
        Both are in the infrared range, which is consistent with the fact that the filters used in this part of the experiment are designed to 
        filter infrared radiation.


    \subsection{Blackbody radiation}

    The setup of the following part is seen in figure \ref{fig:setup2}. It of an electric oven in which a hollow cylinder is heated. 
    Through a hole in the cavity the thermal radiation is emitted. This is a close approximation of a blackbody, since a black body isn't possible 
    in reality.
    \begin{figure}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{../Images/setup2.png}
        \caption{Experimental setup for measurements on blackbody radiation.}
        \label{fig:setup2}
    \end{figure}

        \subsubsection{Stefan-Boltzmann-law} \label{subsec:BlackbodySB}

        Analogously to \ref{sec:SBLeslie}, the voltage generated by the thermopile is measured in an temperature range of 
        Room temperature $= 20^\circ\text{C} \le T \le 400^\circ\text{C}$. The angle at which the thermopile is placed is chosen to be
        $\phi = 0^\circ$, so it is directly facing the hole of the hollow cylinder. The data is again first fitted with the function $U = A (T^b - T_0^b)$, 
        where $T_0$ is the room temperature. The fit is shown in figure \ref{fig:linBlackbody} and the fit parameter turns out to be $b = 4.68 \pm 0.16$.
        \begin{figure}
            \centering
            \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{../Images/fit_U_T_blackbody.png}
            \caption{Voltage generated by the thermopile as a function of the temperature of the blackbody for $\phi = 0^\circ$. The fit used is of the form $U = A (T^b - T_0^b)$, where $T_0$ is the room temperature.}
            \label{fig:linBlackbody}
        \end{figure}

        \noindent
        The obtained value of $b$ is quite far off from the expected value of $b=4$ according to the Stefan-Boltzmann law. 
        This could be due to the fact that the thermopile is not only measuring the thermal radiation emitted by the blackbody, 
        but also the thermal radiation emitted by the environment and reflected on the walls of the cavity, which is not negligible at room temperature. 
        Nevertheless it is still in the same order of magnitude as the expected value, which is a good indication that the Stefan-Boltzmann law is still 
        valid for this setup. \\
        The voltage is again also plotted as a function of the forth power of the temperature as you can see in figure \ref{fig:SBBlackbody2}. 
        
        \begin{figure}[H]
            \centering
            \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{../Images/fit_U_T4_blackbody.png}
            \caption{Voltage generated by the thermopile as a function of the forth power of the temperature of the blackbody for $\phi = 0^\circ$. The fit used is of the form $U = A (T^4 - T_0^4)$, where $T_0$ is the room temperature.}
            \label{fig:SBBlackbody2}
        \end{figure}

        \noindent
        Again here one can see that the data follows a linear trend, which indicates that the Stefan-Boltzmann law is valid for this setup,
        even though the obtained value of $b$ is quite a bit off from the expected value.

        \subsubsection{Lambert's cosine law}

        Since blackbodies radiate without any preferred direction, the intensity of the radiation emitted by a blackbody should follow Lambert's cosine law:
        \begin{equation}
            I(\phi) = I_0 \cos(\phi)
        \end{equation}
        The angle $\phi$ is defined as the angle between the normal vector of the surface and the direction in which the intensity is measured.

        The setup for this part is the same as in \ref{subsec:BlackbodySB} but now the angle $\phi$ at which the thermopile is placed is varied 
        in the range of $0^\circ \le \phi \le 50^\circ$ and the temperature is kept constant at $T = 371^\circ$C. 
        The measured voltage is first normalized to the voltage measured at $\phi = 0^\circ$ and then plotted as a function of the angle $\phi$ 
        and fitted with the trigonometric function $U = a \cos(b \phi + c) + d$. The ideal fit is also included in the plot following the function 
        $U = a \cos(\phi) + b$. The fit is shown in figure \ref{fig:U_phi}.
        \begin{figure}[h]
            \centering
            \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{../Images/lambert_U_phi.png}
            \caption{Voltage generated by the thermopile as a function of the angle $\phi$ for $T = 371^\circ$C. The voltage is normalized to the voltage measured at $\phi = 0^\circ$.}
            \label{fig:U_phi}
        \end{figure}[H]
        \noindent
        Then measured voltage is also plotted as 
        a function of $\cos(\phi)$ and fitted with the function $U = a \cos(\phi) + b$, where $a$ and $b$ are the fit parameters.
        Again the ideal fit is also included in the plot following the function $U = a \cos(\phi) + b$.
        The fit is shown in figure \ref{fig:Lambert}.\\
        \noindent
        In figure \ref{fig:U_phi} one can see that the data follows a cosine trend at small angles, but at larger angles it deviates from the cosine trend.
        This could be due to the fact that at larger angles, the thermopile is not only measuring the radiation emitted by the blackbody, 
        but also the radiation emitted by the environment and reflected on the walls of the cavity, which is not negligible at room temperature. \\
        In figure \ref{fig:Lambert} one can see that the data follows a linear trend and has a similar slope as the ideal fit,
        which is a good indication that the Lambert's cosine law is valid for this setup, even though there are some deviations at larger angles.
        \begin{figure}[H]
            \centering
            \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{../Images/individual_fits_U_cos_phi.png}
            \caption{Voltage generated by the thermopile as a function of $\cos(\phi)$ for $T = 371^\circ$C. The fit used is of the form $U = a \cos(\phi) + b$, where $a$ and $b$ are the fit parameters.}
            \label{fig:Lambert}
        \end{figure}
