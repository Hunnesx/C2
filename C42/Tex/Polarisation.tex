\section{Polarisation of microwaves}
\noindent
\setlength{\parindent}{0pt}

In this part of the experiment, the polarization properties of microwave radiation were investigated 
using a metallic grid placed between transmitter and receiver. The grid acts as a polarization 
filter since electric field components parallel to the metallic wires are suppressed, while 
perpendicular components are transmitted.

The transmitted intensity was measured as a function of the rotation angle $\theta$ of the grid. 
The intensity shows a clear angular dependence, which demonstrates that the emitted microwave 
radiation is linearly polarized.

For an ideal polarization filter, the transmitted intensity follows Malus' law. Since both the 
grid and the receiver contribute to the polarization effect, the measured intensity can approximately 
be described by

\begin{equation}
    I(\theta)
    \propto
    \cos^4(\theta).
\end{equation}

The measured intensities were normalized to the maximum intensity and displayed in a polar plot (fig.\ref{fig:polarisation}).
The intensity reaches its maximum for parallel alignment and decreases significantly for 
perpendicular orientations.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{../Images/7.1.png}
    \caption{Polar plot of the normalized transmitted intensity as a function of the grid angle.}
    \label{fig:polarisation}
\end{figure}

The observed angular dependence confirms the linear polarization of the microwave radiation 
emitted by the Gunn diode.
