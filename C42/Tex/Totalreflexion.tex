\section{Total reflection and frustrated total reflection}
\noindent
\setlength{\parindent}{0pt}


\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{../Images/7.2.png}
    \caption{The setup to observe total reflection. With the two receivers A and B the reflected and transmitted intensity is
measured.}
    \label{fig:tunneling_transmission}
\end{figure}

In this part of the experiment, frustrated total reflection was investigated using two paraffin 
prisms separated by an air gap \ref{fig:tunneling_transmission}. Although total reflection occurs at the interface, a part of the 
electromagnetic wave can penetrate through the gap due to the evanescent field. This behavior is 
analogous to the quantum mechanical tunneling effect.

The transmitted intensity was measured as a function of the prism separation $d$. An exponential 
decrease of the transmitted intensity is expected and can be described by

\begin{equation}
    I(d)
    =
    I_0 \exp\left(-\frac{d}{\delta}\right),
\end{equation}

where $I_0$ is the initial intensity and $\delta$ is the penetration depth of the evanescent field.

The measured data were fitted using the exponential function above. The fit yielded a penetration 
depth of

\begin{equation}
    \delta
    =
    (2.31 \pm 0.14)cm.
\end{equation}

The measured transmission intensity decreases exponentially with increasing prism separation, 
while the reflected intensity increases correspondingly. This behavior confirms the existence of 
the evanescent wave and demonstrates the tunneling effect for microwaves.
